Every loan application tells a story. Behind every approved or rejected request is a human being navigating life — a young adult in City B trying to build something, a senior managing fixed income, a small business owner betting on the future. When you aggregate 30,000 of those stories into a dataset, patterns emerge that no individual case could reveal.

This dashboard was built not just to track numbers, but to ask harder questions: Who is carrying the most debt? Where does default risk actually concentrate? Are low-income borrowers being systematically over-exposed? Let me walk you through what the data showed — and what it quietly hides.

The Risk Paradox — Low-Income Borrowers Carry the Most Debt

Perhaps the single most important finding in this entire dashboard sits in the Risk Dashboard panel. Loan amounts by income group show a distribution that should keep any credit risk officer up at night.

The low-income group holds virtually all of the portfolio's loan value ($1.06bn), while medium and high-income borrowers account for a negligible fraction. This is an extreme concentration that points to one of two realities: either this lender is a microfinance or underserved-market institution, or the income segmentation data has classification issues worth investigating.

Either way, from a risk management standpoint, this concentration in a single income band creates enormous systemic exposure. A localized economic shock — job losses, inflation spikes, policy changes — affecting low-income earners would essentially stress the entire portfolio simultaneously.

Bank Type Breakdown — Private Banks Lead

The portfolio splits across three bank types. Private banks (P) lead with $0.62bn, government banks (G) hold $0.33bn, and a small unclassified segment accounts for $0.12bn.

The 12% unclassified segment is a data quality red flag. In risk modelling, unclassified lender records are essentially "known unknowns" — they exist in the portfolio but can't be properly stress-tested, regulated, or benchmarked. A data cleaning sprint to resolve these would immediately improve the portfolio's analytical integrity.

Data Cleaning Steps Applied

The raw dataset required several preparation steps before it was analytically sound: removal of null loan amount records; standardization of gender labels; normalization of income group classifications; resolution of duplicate Customer ID entries; and data type formatting for interest rate fields.

Three interactive filters were configured on the final dashboard — City Category, Primary Bank Type, and Interest Rate range — enabling executives to drill into any segment without navigating to a separate report page.

What the Data Is Asking You to Do Next

Good analytics doesn't end at the dashboard. Here are four concrete next steps this analysis demands:

1. Conduct a debt-to-income audit on the low-income cohort. With $1.06bn in loans on a $101M/month income base, the repayment math needs to be stress-tested under 2%, 5%, and 10% income shock scenarios.

2. Investigate the Young Adult female lending gap. The gender gap almost disappears in older age groups — which means the barrier is generational, not structural. Targeted outreach and product design for young adult women could expand the market meaningfully.

3. Resolve the 12% unclassified bank type segment. These records are flying blind in the portfolio. A data cleanse sprint — even a partial one — would improve risk modelling accuracy immediately.

4. Explore geographic expansion beyond City B. 72% customer concentration in one city is a single-point-of-failure risk. Cities A and C are underserved — which is either a risk mitigation opportunity or a market growth one, depending on your strategy.
